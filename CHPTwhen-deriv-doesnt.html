    Chapter 16 When the Derivative Doesn’t Exist

    I turn away with fright and horror from the lamentable evil of functions which do not have derivatives.
    ―Charles Hermite (\(1822\) - \(1901\)) 1 
    The brilliant Cerebron, .\ .\ .\ discovered three distinct kinds of dragon: the mythical, the chimerical, and the purely hypothetical. They were all, one might say, nonexistent, but each non-existed in an entirely different way.
    ―Stanislaw Lem (\(1921\) - \(2006\)) 2 
    In [cross-reference to target(s) "SUBSECunderl-deriv" missing or not unique] we saw that those points where the derivative doesn’t exist are possible optimal points but we didn’t pursue this any further at the time. However we now need to re-examine the non-existence of derivatives.
    We also observed in [cross-reference to target(s) "SUBSECunderl-deriv" missing or not unique] that derivatives fail to exist at points where the Principle of Local Linearity does not hold. This is true, but the only way we currently have to determine if the Principle of Local Linearity holds is to look at a graph. While graphs are useful, we have learned that we should not put all of our faith in what we conclude from them.
    On the other hand, by [cross-reference to target(s) "DEFDerivative" missing or not unique] the derivative of a function is given by a limit, and from our work in Chapter 12 we know that not all limits exist. For example, if we try to compute the value of \(f^\prime(2)\) for some function and we find that \(\tlimit{h}{0}{\frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h}}\) is meaningless then \(f\) is not differentiable at \(x=2\text{.}\) In other words, \(f^\prime(2)\) does not exist.
    So, the limit definition gives us a computational tool we can use to decide the question of differentiability which, as we saw in [cross-reference to target(s) "SUBSECoptim-abstr-probl" missing or not unique], is crucial to finding possible optimal transition points of a function.
    In our comments following Example~\ 9.55 we listed several functions which are not differentiable at \(x=0\text{.}\) Of these, the Absolute Value function, \(f(x)=\abs{x}\text{,}\) is probably the simplest to work with, so we will start there.

    Example 16.1. The Absolute Value Function.

    The Absolute Value function is usually introduced with some vague statement like, \centerline{“The absolute value of a number is just the positive version of the number,”} or \centerline{“\(\abs{x-a}\) gives the length of the line segment between \(x\) and \(a\text{.}\)”} These are both true statements and are OK as intuitive definitions, but we will need something more precise from now on. The formal definition of the Absolute Value function is: \begin{mydefinition}[The Absolute Value Function] The Absolute Value of \(x\) is: \(\abs{x}= \begin{cases} x\amp\text{ if } x\ge0 -x\amp\text{ if } x\lt 0 \end{cases}\text{.}\) \end{mydefinition} \begin{wrapfigure}[10]{r}{2in} \vskip-3mm \captionsetup{labelformat=empty} \centerline{\includegraphics*[height=1.5in,width=2in]{../Figures/AbsVal}} \label{fig:AbsVal} \end{wrapfigure} The graph of the Absolute Value function is given at the right. Notice that \(f(x)=\abs{x}\) is defined at \(x=0\) since \(\abs{0}=0\text{,}\) but as we indicated in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONunderl-deriv" missing or not unique] the Principle of Local Linearity does not hold at \(x=0\) so we suspect that the Absolute Value function is not differentiable at \(x=0\text{.}\) But we now have the ability to confirm our suspicion analytically so let’s take a look at this using the limit definition of the derivative. The derivative of \(\abs{x}\) at \(x\) will be the value of the limit \(\tlimit{h}{0}{\frac{\abs{x+h}-\abs{x}}{h}}\) so the derivative of \(\abs{x}\) at \(x=0\) will be:
    \begin{equation*} \limit{h}{0}{\frac{\abs{0+h}-\abs{0}}{h}}= \limit{h}{0}{\frac{\abs{h}}{h}}. \end{equation*}
    OK, but what is this limit? Don’t jump to conclusions. Think about this carefully for a few minutes. What do you think \(\tlimit{h}{0}{\frac{\abs{h}}{h}}\) is equal to? First suppose that \(h\) is approaching\aside{To say that \(h\) is “approaching” \(0\) suggests that \(h\) is sliding along the horizontal axis toward the origin. We encourage you to think of limits this way. It is a good intuitive approach. However, as we will see in [cross-reference to target(s) "CHAPTERcha:formal-limits" missing or not unique] it isn’t quite what is really happening.} zero from the positive side. Recall that in ~ 12.3 we invented the notation \(\rlimit{h}{0} \) for exactly this situation. In this case \(h\) is positive so \(\abs{h}=h\) and
    \begin{equation*} \rlimit{h}{0}{\frac{\abs{h}}{h}}=\rlimit{h}{0}{\frac{h}{h}}=\rlimit{h}{0}{1}=1 \end{equation*}
    which says that the right-hand limit will be one. Now suppose \(h\) is very small and negative. In this case \(h\) is negative so \(\abs{h}=-h\) and
    \begin{equation*} \llimit{h}{0}{\frac{\abs{h}}{h}}=\llimit{h}{0}{\frac{-h}{h}}=\llimit{h}{0}{-1}=-1 \end{equation*}
    which says that the left-hand limit will be negative one. But since \(\frac{\abs{h}}{h}\) can’t be close to \(1\) and close to \(-1\) simultaneously we can not find a value for \(\tlimit{h}{0}{\frac{\abs{0+h}-\abs{0}}{h}}\) which is correct for both positive and negative values of \(h\text{.}\) In other words this limit does not exist. Therefore \(\eval{\dfdx{\abs{x}}{x}}{x}{0}\) does not exist. \begin{ProficiencyDrill} According to ~ 9.57, since \(\dfdxat{(\abs{x})}{x}{0}\) does not exist, the value \(x=0\) is a possible transition point for the Absolute Value function. So there might be a local extremum of \(f(x)=\abs{x}\) at \(x=0\text{.}\) Is there? \end{ProficiencyDrill}

    Example 16.3.

    A slightly less intuitive example is the function \(f(x)=\frac{x}{x}\text{.}\) It is tempting to claim that this is really just \(f(x)=1\) since “anything divided by itself is one” but that claim is not true. As we saw in [cross-reference to target(s) "DIGRESSIONDivideByZero" missing or not unique] division by zero is undefined, regardless of the numerator. So the expression \(\frac00\) is undefined, and therefore the function \(f(x)=\frac{x}{x}\) is not defined at \(x=0\text{.}\) It is also tempting to try to invoke \lhop ’s Rule, since it is true that
    \begin{equation*} \limit{x}{0}{f(x)}= \limit{x}{0}{\frac{x}{x}} = \limit{x}{0}{1}=1. \end{equation*}
    But this only says that the limit of \(f(x)\) is equal to one, not that \(f(x)\) itself is equal to one, at \(x=0\text{.}\) The statement \(\limit{x}{0}{\frac{x}{x}} = \limit{x}{0}{1}\) explicitly excludes the point \(x=0\) from consideration. This is why when we discussed indeterminate forms in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECmore-indet-forms" missing or not unique] we were very careful to write \(\frac{\approach{0}}{\approach{0}}\) instead of \(\frac00\text{.}\) We needed to emphasize that we specifically do not allow the denominator to be equal to zero.

    \begin{wrapfigure}[]{r}{2in} \captionsetup{labelformat=empty} \centerline{\includegraphics*[height=1in,width=2in]{../Figures/AbsSqrtX}} \label{fig:Astroid3} \end{wrapfigure} It is intuitively clear that derivative of the function \(f(x)=\sqrt{\abs{x}}\) (at the right) is not defined at \(x=0\text{.}\) We will confirm our intuition using [cross-reference to target(s) "DEFINITIONDerivative" missing or not unique]. That is, we’ll try to compute the derivative of \(f(x)\) at \(x=0\) using [cross-reference to target(s) "DEFINITIONDerivative" missing or not unique] and see what goes wrong. The existence of the derivative of \(f(x)\) at \(x=0\) is equivalent to the existence of the limit \(\tlimit{h}{0}{\frac{f(0+h)-f(0)}{h}}\text{,}\) so we will try to compute this limit and see what happens.
    \begin{equation*} f^\prime(0) \stackrel{?}{=} \limit{h}{0}{\frac{f(0+h)-f(0)}{h}}=\limit{h}{0}{\frac{\sqrt{\abs{h}}}{h}}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{embeddedproblem-enumerate} \label{PROBLEMLRLim1overAbsh} \begin{enumerate}[label={ (\alph*)}] \begin{multicols}{2} \item Show that \(\rlimit{h}{0}{\frac{\sqrt{\abs{h}}}{h}}=\infty\text{.}\) \item Show that \(\llimit{h}{0}{\frac{\sqrt{\abs{h}}}{h}}=-\infty\text{.}\) \end{multicols} \end{enumerate} \end{embeddedproblem-enumerate} [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMLRLim1overAbsh" missing or not unique] shows, \(\frac{\sqrt{\abs{h}}}{h}\) increases without bound as \(h\) approaches zero from the right, and decreases without bound as \(h\) approaches zero from the left. Since neither of these limits even exists it follows that \(\tlimit{h}{0}{\frac{\sqrt{\abs{h}}}{h}}\) also does not exist, and therefore \(f^\prime(0)\) is not defined\aside{As a verbal shorthand most people will just say that the right- and left-hand limits are not equal and so \(f^\prime(0)\) does not exist. Be sure you understand why this is, strictly speaking, incorrect.}.

    Example 16.6. The Heaviside Function.

    \begin{wrapfigure}[]{r}{2.5in} \captionsetup{labelformat=empty} \vskip-3mm \centerline{\includegraphics*[height=1.7in,width=2.5in]{../Figures/Heaviside2}} \label{fig:Heaviside2} \end{wrapfigure} Problem 16.2 shows that the derivative of the Absolute Value function (shown at the right) is:
    \begin{equation*} H(x)= \begin{cases} 1\amp x\gt0 -1\amp x\lt0 \end{cases}. \end{equation*}
    Notice that it is not defined at \(x=0\text{.}\) We call \(H(x)\) the Heaviside function in honor of \href{https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Heaviside/}{Oliver Heaviside} (1850-1925). Simple as it is, Heaviside’s function is a fundamental tool in signal processing, control theory, and the solution of differential equations.

    Problem 16.7.

    (a)

    Use [cross-reference to target(s) "DEFINITIONDerivative" missing or not unique] to show that \(H^\prime(x)=0\) when \(x\ne0\)

    (b)

    Explain why the result in part (a) does not contradict Problem 15.15
    We first introduced one-sided limits in [cross-reference to target(s) "SUBSECvertical-asymptotes" missing or not unique]. At the time our concern was to locate vertical asymptotes so we only looked at limits that were equal to either positive or negative infinity. We see now that these limits can take any value and that they are more fundamental than they first appeared to be. Since we didn’t provide a formal definition earlier we will do so now.

    Definition 16.8. One-sided Limits.

    If \(R\) is a real number (or \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\)) and
    \begin{equation*} \rlimit{x}{a}{f(x)} = R \end{equation*}
    we say that \(R\) is the right-hand limit of \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\text{.}\) If \(L\) is a real number (or \(-\infty\) or \(-\infty\)) and
    \begin{equation*} \llimit{x}{a}{f(x)} = L \end{equation*}
    we say that \(L\) is the left-hand limit of \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    Because \(f(x)\) can’t simultaneously approach two different numbers, if the left and right handed limits both exist but do not agree then \(\limit{x}{a}{f(x)}\) doesn’t exist. This fact will be a useful tool for us later so we state it as a theorem.

    Theorem 16.9. One Sided Derivatives.

    \(\limit{x}{a}{f(x)}\) exists if and only if \(\rlimit{x}{a}{f(x)} = \llimit{x}{a}{f(x)}.\) In that case all three limits are equal.
    Theorem~ 16.9 requires proof but we do not have the tools to prove it here. The appropriate tools will be developed in [cross-reference to target(s) "CHAPTERcha:formal-limits" missing or not unique]. Proving this fact is the last problem in this textbook.

    Section 16.1 One Sided Derivatives

    Since limits may be right-hand or left-hand and the derivative is defined as a limit, it follows that derivatives can be right- or left-hand as well. We will denote the right-hand and left-hand derivatives at \(x=a\) with the notation: \(\rprime{f}(a),\) and \(\lprime{f}(a),\) respectively:

    Definition 16.10. One Sided Derivatives.

    Given a function, \(f(x)\text{,}\) defined at a point \(a\text{:}\)
    1. \(f\) is said to have a right-hand derivative at \(x=a\) if the limit
      \begin{equation*} \rprime{f}(a)=\rlimit{h}{0}{\textstyle\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}} \end{equation*}
      exists.
    2. \(f\) is said to have a left-hand derivative at \(x=a\) if the limit
      \begin{equation*} \lprime{f}(a)=\llimit{h}{0}{\textstyle\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}} \end{equation*}
      exists.
    From Theorem~\ 16.9 and [cross-reference to target(s) "DEFone-sided-deriv" missing or not unique] we see that the derivative of a function \(f\text{,}\) at a point \(x\) exists if and only if the right- and left-hand derivatives both exist, and are equal.

    Corollary 16.11.

    If \(\rprime{f}(x)= \lprime{f}(x)\text{,}\) then \(f(x)\) is differentiable at \(x\) and \(f^\prime(x)=\rprime{f}(x)= \lprime{f}(x)\text{.}\)
    At zero both the right- and left-hand derivatives of the Absolute Value function \(f(x)=\abs{x}\) exist since
    \begin{equation*} \rprime{f}(0)=\rlimit{h}{0}{\frac{\abs{0+h}-\abs{0}}{h}}=\rlimit{h}{0}{\frac{h}{h}}=1, \end{equation*}
    and

    but \(f^\prime(0)\) does not exist because \(-1\neq1\text{.}\)

    In [cross-reference to target(s) "SECoptim-clos-interv" missing or not unique] we found that the possible transition points come in three “flavors:” (1) points where the derivative is zero, (2) points where the derivative is undefined, and, (3) if the function is defined on a closed interval, the endpoints of the interval. We remarked at the time that it seemed odd that the third condition does not involve the derivative while the other two do.
    With our new, deeper understanding of differentiation we can show that, in fact, there are really only two kinds of possible transition points, and both can be identified using the derivative. It turns out that the endpoints of an interval are really just points of non-differentiability.
    To see this consider any function, \(f(x)\) which is continuous on the closed interval \([a,b]\) and differentiable on the open interval \((a,b)\text{.}\) From Corollary~\ 16.11 we know that \(f^\prime(b)\) only exists if \(\rprime{f}(b)\) and \(\lprime{f}(b)\) both exist\aside{They also need to be equal, but that is irrelevant right now.}.
    But the right-hand limit at \(b\) doesn’t exist. To see this, observe that \(f\) is only defined on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Therefore when \(h>0\) the expression \(f(b+h)\) asks us to evaluate \(f\) at a point outside its domain, which is meaningless. Thus
    \begin{equation*} \rprime{f}(b)=\rlimit{h}{0}{\frac{\CancelToRed{\text{meaningless}}{f(b+h)}-f(b)}{h}} \end{equation*}
    does not exist. A similar argument shows that \(\lprime{f}(a)\) does not exist.
    Therefore \(f^\prime\) does not exist at the endpoints of a closed interval. As a result the possible transition points of \(f\) are only those places where the derivative is zero, or undefined, because the latter category includes the endpoints of closed intervals.
