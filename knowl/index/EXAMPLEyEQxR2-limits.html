Example 13.2.

Suppose \(y=x^2\text{.}\) We would like to compute the derivative (slope of the line tangent to the graph) of \(y\) at the point \(x=2\) by the the procedure indicated above.
Before we start, observe that from our work with differentials we know what we are expecting to get. It is
\begin{equation*} y^\prime(2)=\left.\dfdx{y}{x}\right|_{x=2}=\bigeval{2x}{x}{2}=2\cdot2=4. \end{equation*}
Taking \(x_1=2.1\) and \(x_2=1.9,\) gives
\begin{align*} \frac{y(2.1)-y(2)}{2.1-2}\amp{}\gt y^\prime(2)\gt\frac{y(1.9)-y(2)}{1.9-2} \\\\ \frac{(2.1)^2-(2)^2}{.1}\amp{}\gt y^\prime(2)\gt\frac{(1.9)^2-y(2)}{.9}\\ 4.1\amp{}\gt y^\prime(2)\gt3.9. \end{align*}
So our proposed procedure seems to be heading us in the right direction. If we take \(x_3=2.01\) and \(x_4=1.99\) we get
\begin{equation*} 4.01\gt y^\prime(2)\gt3.99. \end{equation*}
We say that \(y^\prime(2)\) is bounded, and that the numbers \(4.01\) and \(3.99\) are the bounds.
