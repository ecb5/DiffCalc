If we restrict \(x\) to negative values then we see that\aside{Note that the sense of the inequality has changed from equation (12.1)). It is now “less than.”}
\begin{equation} \limitX{x}{0}{\frac1x}{x\lt 0}=-\infty.\tag{12.2} \end{equation}
The limit in equation (12.2)) is called a left–hand limit and is read aloud as “the limit as \(x\) approaches zero from the left, of one over \(x\) equals negative infinity.” Right- and left-hand limits are generically called one-sided limits. One-sided limits are important theoretical tools but their theoretical value is not important to us right now. We will discuss them in more depth in Section 16.2.
