If we replace \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) with their tangent lines at \(x=a\) we have
\begin{align} \limit{x}{a}{\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}} \amp = \limit{x}{a}{\frac{f(a)+f^\prime(a)(x-a)}{g(a)+g^\prime(a)(x-a)}}\notag\\ \amp = \limit{x}{a}{\frac{0+f^\prime(a)(x-a)}{0+g^\prime(a)(x-a)}}\notag\\ \limit{x}{a}{\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}}\amp = \frac{f^\prime(a)}{g^\prime(a)}\tag{12.3} \end{align}
(We need to assume that \(g^\prime(a)\neq0\text{.}\) Do you see why?) This is consistent with all of our examples above so it appears that the Principle of Local Linearity has led us in the right direction.
