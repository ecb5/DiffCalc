Example 16.1.0.6.

A slightly less intuitive example is the function \(f(x)=\frac{x}{x}\text{.}\) It is tempting to claim that this is really just \(f(x)=1\) since “anything divided by itself is one” but that claim is not true. As we saw in Dividing By Zero division by zero is undefined, regardless of the numerator. So the expression \(\frac00\) is undefined, and therefore the function \(f(x)=\frac{x}{x}\) is not defined at \(x=0\text{.}\) It is also tempting to try to invoke L’Hôpital’s Rule, since it is true that
\begin{equation*} \limit{x}{0}{f(x)}= \limit{x}{0}{\frac{x}{x}} = \limit{x}{0}{1}=1. \end{equation*}
But this only says that the limit of \(f(x)\) is equal to one, not that \(f(x)\) itself is equal to one, at \(x=0\text{.}\) As we’ve seen the statement \(\limit{x}{0}{\frac{x}{x}} = \limit{x}{0}{1}\) explicitly excludes the point \(x=0\) from consideration. This is why when we discussed indeterminate forms in Section 12.4 we were very careful to write \(\frac{\approach{0}}{\approach{0}}\) instead of \(\frac00\text{.}\) We needed to emphasize that we specifically do not allow the denominator to be equal to zero.
