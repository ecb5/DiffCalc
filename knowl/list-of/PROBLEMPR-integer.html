Problem 4.3.5.2. The Product Rule for Positive Integers.

By custom, differentials are written at the end of each term where they appear in a given computation so the Product Rule is usually written as
\begin{equation*} \dx{(xy)}=x(\dx{y})+y(\dx{x}). \end{equation*}
But this is just a custom so it is not strictly necessary. We could also use the Product Rule in the form
\begin{equation*} \dx{(xy)}=x(\dx{y})+(\dx{x})y. \end{equation*}
(a)
Use the Product Rule twice to show that
\begin{equation*} \dx{(xyz)}=xy(\dx{z})+x(\dx{y})z+(\dx{x})yz, \end{equation*}
Hint.
The Product Rule requires two factors. But we have three: \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\text{.}\) We can fix this with the substitution \(\alpha=yz\text{.}\) Then \(\dx{(xyz)}=\dx{(x\alpha)}.\) Now use the Product Rule.

(c)
What does the Product Rule look like for \(\dx{(x_1x_2x_3\cdots x_n)}?\)
(d)
Use the result of part (c) with \(x_1=x_2=\cdots = x_n =x\) to show that
\begin{equation} \dx\left(x^n\right)=nx^{n-1}\dx{x}.\tag{4.5} \end{equation}
