Example 14.4.
Suppose
\begin{equation*} f(x)= \begin{cases} 2x \amp \text{if } x\neq3\\ 10 \amp \text{if } x=3 \end{cases}. \end{equation*}
Then
\begin{equation*} \limit{x}{3}{f(x)}= 6. \end{equation*}
In particular the limit is not \(10\text{.}\) Here is how we would evaluate this limit using the tools we currently have at our disposal. We’re interested in the limit as \(x\rightarrow3\) so in particular we do not need to consider the case when \(x=3\text{.}\) But as long as \(x\neq3\) we have \(f(x)=2x\) so
\begin{equation*} \limit{x}{3}{f(x)}= \limit{x}{3}{2x}. \end{equation*}
As \(x\) gets close to \(3\) it is clear that \(2x\) gets close to \(6\text{.}\) Therefore
\begin{equation*} \limit{x}{3}{f(x)}= 6. \end{equation*}
Notice that our reasoning is a little vague in the last step because we had to resort to the phase “gets close to,” and we know from our work in ~ 12.4 that this is not a precise phrase.
