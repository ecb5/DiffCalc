Example 6.33.
We can now use the new rules in conjuction with the General Differentiation Rules from ChapterÂ 4
(a)
Suppose \(y=3\theta+2\sin(\theta).\) Then \(\dx{y} = \dx(3\theta+2\sin(\theta))=(3+2\cos(\theta))\dx{\theta}. \)
(b)
Suppose \(y=\textcolor{blue}{(t^3-2t^2)}\textcolor{red}{\cos(t^2-t)}.\) Letting \(\textcolor{red}{\theta=t^2-t}\) we have
\begin{align*} \dx{y} \amp = \textcolor{blue}{(t^3-2t^2)}\textcolor{red}{\dx{(\cos(\theta)})}+\textcolor{red}{\cos(\theta)}\textcolor{blue}{\dx{(t^3-2t^2)}}\\ \amp = \textcolor{blue}{(t^3-2t^2)}\textcolor{red}{(-\sin(\theta))\dx{\theta}}+\textcolor{red}{\cos(\theta)}\textcolor{blue}{\dx{(t^3-2t^2)}}\\ \amp = \textcolor{blue}{(t^3-2t^2)}\textcolor{red}{(-\sin(t^2-t))(2t-1)\dx{t}} + \textcolor{blue}{\cos(t^2-t)(3t^2-4t)\dx{t}}\\ \amp = -\left[(t^3-2t^2)(2t-1)\sin(t^2-t) + (3t^2-4t)\cos(t^2-t)\right]\dx{t}. \end{align*}
(c)
Suppose \(y=\sin(2\theta).\) Then \(\dx{y} = \cos(2\theta)\dx{(2\theta)}= 2\cos(2\theta)\dx{\theta}.\)
(d)
Suppose \(y=\sin(\theta^2).\) Then \(\dx{y} = \cos(\theta^2)\dx(\theta^2)= 2\theta\sin(\theta^2)\dx{\theta}.\)
(e)
Suppose \(y=\sin(k-\theta),\) where \(k\) is constant. Then
\begin{equation*} \dx{y} = \dx(\sin(k-\theta)) = \cos(k-\theta)\dx(k-\theta)=-\cos(k-\theta)\dx{\theta} \end{equation*}
