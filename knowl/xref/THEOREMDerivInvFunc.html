Theorem 14.49. The Derivative of Inverse Functions.

Suppose that \begin{enumerate} \begin{multicols}{2} \item \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{,}\) \item \(f(a)=b\text{,}\) \item \(f^\prime(a)\neq0\text{,}\) \item \(\inverse{f}\) is continuous at\aside{In fact, this follows from the continuity of \(f\) at \(x=a\text{.}\) We do not have all of the tools necessary to prove this so we must include it in the assumptions of our theorem.} \(x=b\text{.}\) \end{multicols} \end{enumerate} Then the inverse of \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=b\) and
\begin{equation*} \DD\left(\inverse{f}(b)\right)=\frac{1}{f^\prime\left(\inverse{f}(b)\right)}. \end{equation*}
